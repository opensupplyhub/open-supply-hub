# **About This Guide**

This document establishes writing and communications standards for all OS Hub external-facing content. It covers our brand identity and messaging framework, general voice and tone guidelines, audience-specific approaches, and channel standards for our newsletter, blog, LinkedIn, and press releases.

Use it as a reference whenever you are creating, reviewing, or approving content. It is a living document — flag gaps or suggestions to the Communications team.

# **Brand Identity at a Glance**

## **Our Name**

Getting the name right is the most basic brand standard:

* **Full name:** Open Supply Hub

* **Short form:** OS Hub — acceptable after the full name has been used once

* **Never use:** OSH, OSHub, the hub, OS-Hub, or any other variation

* **Platform reference:** “the OS Hub platform” or “the platform” in subsequent mentions

* **Describe us as:** “a global open data platform” or “a non-profit platform”

*Note:  In logo/wordmark contexts the name is set in ALL CAPS. In running body text use title case: “Open Supply Hub.”*

# **Voice & Tone**

## **Our Voice**

OS Hub’s voice is concise, clear, and empathetic. Across all channels, it should feel:

* **Clear and direct:** We respect our readers’ time. Say what you mean without unnecessary jargon.

* **Purposeful:** Every piece of content should have a reason to exist and a clear takeaway.

* **Inclusive and accessible:** Diverse global audience. Avoid insider language; explain acronyms on first use.

* **Human and warm:** We work on serious issues, but we are not stiff. Personality is welcome.

* **Credible:** Back up claims with data. Link to sources. Let the evidence speak.

* **Empathetic:** Acknowledge the complexity and difficulty of the challenges our audience faces.

* **Precise over vague:** Specificity is a form of honesty, and honesty is our competitive advantage. We don’t say “comprehensive supply chain intelligence.” We say “facility-level data across more than 2.5 million production locations.” Numbers and named capabilities beat adjectives every time.

* **Outcome-led, not feature-led:** Lead with what becomes possible, not what the product does. Readers don’t want “enriched data” — they want to spot a risky supplier before it becomes a news story, or find a partner working on the same problem.

* **Confident without being promotional:** We don’t use superlatives we haven’t earned. No “industry-leading,” “best-in-class,” or “most powerful” without evidence. What we can say is specific and defensible: “the world’s largest open database of supply chain production locations.” That lands harder than any adjective.

* **Mission-anchored without being preachy:** The mission is the frame, not the pitch. We don’t open every communication with a values statement. Our audiences have missions too — they need OS Hub to help them act on those missions practically. Surface the mission where it adds credibility (e.g. why our data is more trustworthy than a for-profit competitor’s), not as a substitute for a substantive argument.

* **Honest about what we don’t yet have:** OS Hub’s credibility depends on people trusting what we say. If a data category isn’t available yet, say so. If coverage in a specific region is limited, say so. Overclaiming — even by omission — is a trust-erosion event we cannot afford.

### **Editorial sensitivities**

* Do not editorialize about OS Hub’s own work. Avoid phrases like “exciting” or “proud” in body copy — let the work speak for itself. Context: “We partnered with X to launch Y” not “We’re thrilled to announce our exciting new partnership with X.”

* The OS Hub brand does not have emotions. “We’re excited” and “We’re proud” are phrases to avoid in brand voice. They read as corporate filler and undercut our credibility. Individual team members writing blog posts or personal LinkedIn content may express their own feelings — that is different from brand voice.

* Avoid white savior framing and paternalistic language. OS Hub does not “help” or “empower” vulnerable communities — we build infrastructure that enables action. The workers, communities, and organizations in our data are not passive recipients; they are agents in their own right. Be careful with “empowering” in particular: it almost always implies the speaker holds the power and is conferring it, which is the opposite of what open data is meant to do. Note: “empowering” is disproportionately applied to people in the Global South — this compounds the problem.

* Cultural sensitivity: OS Hub works in more than 200 countries. Content involving specific geographies, communities, or political situations should be reviewed carefully. When in doubt, consult Nandita Shivakumar, who has documented specific cultural and regional sensitivities for OS Hub content. See the internal cultural guide (linked in the onboarding doc) before writing about regions where you are uncertain.

* **Crisis management/response:** The OS Hub team and the communities we work with often deal with global, national or regional crises in the form of extreme weather events or political situations, among other possibilities. Our typical decision matrix for communicating as an organization in any particular situation follows:

* If it is supply chain-related and we can reasonably create a resource/map that would help, we do that and use that as our way of communicating in that moment

* If it’s an issue that doesn’t touch us/supply chains, we don’t make statements or try to address it.

* If it’s a crisis affecting a large portion of the world/our community, we don’t try to generate/put out content in that time and let the focus stay on that issue on social media, etc.


### **What we avoid**

These patterns appear in early drafts and should be caught in review.

* **Urgency theater:** Phrases like “you need this before CSDDD hits” are the hallmark of vendors who don’t understand their audience’s situation. We create urgency through relevance, not fear. Our platform is built to sustain beyond any single regulation.

* **Overclaiming on coverage:** Say what the data actually includes. “Facility-level emissions data from a curated set of founding partners” is accurate and valuable. Suggesting encyclopedic coverage that doesn’t yet exist is not. The network is designed to grow — say that clearly and let it stand.

* **“Unique” or “first” without specifics:** “Unique data” means nothing. “The only open, persistent, sector-agnostic facility identifier in the ecosystem” means something. Earn the claim or don’t make it.

* **Apologizing for commercial pricing:** OS Hub is a non-profit, not a charity for commercial buyers. Premium services carry real prices because they deliver real value. Don’t soften pricing conversations with excessive mission language.

## **The OS Hub Piano — Structuring Communications**

Don’t lead with features or data points. Structure content to move people through three stages:

* **01 Inspire:** Open with the possibility — what if there was a way to start solving multiple problems, find new collaborators, target resources better?

* **02 Explore:** Show examples. Case studies, user stories, real-world impact.

* **03 Engage:** Explain how it works and how they can get involved.

*Note:  This applies especially to LinkedIn posts, blog introductions, presentations, and press releases. It’s more effective than leading with features.*

## **Grammar & Style Basics**

OS Hub follows AP Style (Associated Press Stylebook) as its baseline framework for all external writing. This is the standard for journalism, PR, and non-profit communications. When AP Style does not address a specific case, default to Merriam-Webster.

Write in American English throughout — use American spellings (e.g., “organization” not “organisation,” “labor” not “labour,” “analyze” not “analyse”, “program” not “programme”).

*Note:  OS Hub is an international team with many British English speakers. When in doubt, check Merriam-Webster. Set your spell-checker to English (US).*

### **AP Style rules that apply directly**

* Use sentence case for headings — AP is lowercase-heavy. Exception: press release headlines use title case. Note: this is a deliberate change from current OS Hub practice (existing materials largely use title case). New content should follow sentence case going forward; existing materials will be updated over time.

* Spell out acronyms on first use with the acronym immediately after in parentheses: “Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG)” then “ESG.”

* Numerals: use figures for 10 and above; spell out one through nine. Exception: always use figures for percentages (“5%” not “five percent”), statistics, ages, and data points regardless of size (“5 million production locations”).

* Use % (not “percent”) in all contexts — AP now permits this universally.

* Job titles: lowercase when they follow a name (“Natalie Grillon, chief executive officer of Open Supply Hub”). Capitalize only when the title immediately precedes the name as a formal title (“CEO Natalie Grillon”).

* Dates: Month Day, Year format in body text (e.g., April 29, 2026). Abbreviate months with six or more letters when used with a specific date (Jan., Feb., Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov., Dec.).

* Use active voice: “Our users mapped 2.5 million locations” not “2.5 million locations were mapped by our users.”

* Avoid hedging language: instead of “may potentially help,” write “helps.”

* Avoid superlatives like “groundbreaking” or “revolutionary” — let the facts make the case.

* Hyperlinks: use descriptive anchor text — never “click here” or a bare URL.

* Em dashes: use a space on each side — like this — not closed up. Use sparingly in external content; em dashes have become closely associated with AI-generated writing, which can undermine authenticity. Prefer commas, colons, or a new sentence where possible.

*AI-generated content: AI tools are permitted to assist with drafting, but all content must be reviewed, edited, and owned by a human team member before publishing. The goal is for OS Hub content to sound like OS Hub — specific, mission-grounded, and free of generic AI patterns (including overuse of em dashes, filler phrases like “it’s worth noting,” and vague impact language). If you use AI to draft, edit the output to reflect OS Hub’s voice as defined in this guide.*

* Rounding numbers: When referencing the platform’s production location count in the newsletter, social posts, or other external communications, use a rounded figure (e.g., “more than 2.5 million production locations”) rather than an exact number. The exact count changes frequently; a round number is accurate enough and more readable. Confirm the current rounded figure before publishing.

### **OS Hub exceptions to AP Style**

| Rule | AP default | OS Hub exception |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Oxford comma | Omit before the final item in a series | Always use: “brands, retailers, and civil society” — clarity matters more than press convention |
| Numerals in narrative | Figures for 10+, words for 1–9 | In long-form narrative stories and blog posts, you may spell out numbers up to 10 for a more human, readable flow |
| Non-profit | "nonprofit" (one word, no hyphen) | OS Hub uses “non-profit” (hyphenated) as the organization’s preferred style |

**\! Always verify statistics before publishing — production location counts and contributor numbers change regularly.**

# **Channel-Specific Guidelines**

## **Newsletter**

OS Hub publishes a monthly newsletter via HubSpot. It is our primary direct channel to stakeholders.

### **Format & Length**

* Keep section intros short — one to three sentences. Link out for detail.

* Use inline hyperlinks rather than listing URLs at the end of paragraphs.

* Bold all hyperlinks to improve visibility.

* Each story should feel self-contained and scannable.

* Avoid dense paragraphs — if a section runs more than four lines, consider splitting or cutting.

### **Tone**

* More concise than LinkedIn — punchy and community-oriented.

* Warm but efficient. Readers should feel informed in under two minutes.

* Avoid repeating content that will appear in a full LinkedIn post or blog; tease and link instead.

### **Content Types**

Typical sections: new data/platform updates, partnerships and milestones, events and speaking engagements, press coverage, job postings, and calls to action.

### **Visual Standards**

Use brand hex codes when customizing templates in HubSpot. The Donate button and key CTAs should use brand purple (\#8428FA) or navy (\#0D1128). Check hex codes even for black and white elements.

### **Workflow**

* Draft in HubSpot; proofread and check all links in test email; share a preview link with minimum two (ideally three) other members of the team for copy-edit review before sending (at least one reviewer should be outside of the US to make sure the content is for a global audience).

* Target send date: last week of the month.

* Solicit content from the team via \#comms-laurens-content-treasure-trove at least two weeks before send.

* Data section: pull new production locations added to the platform that month (see Slack video guide).

## **Blog**

The OS Hub blog is our primary long-form content channel, supporting SEO, thought leadership, and stakeholder education. It lives on WordPress and is publicly accessible.

### **Authors**

Blog posts can be written by OS Hub staff, guest contributors (e.g. partner organizations or sector experts), or a named team member sharing a personal perspective. The distinction between a blog post and a case study matters: case studies document what happened and the impact; blog posts share a viewpoint, argument, or analysis. Use the blog when someone has a perspective to offer, not just a story to tell.

Because blog posts are attributed to individual authors, they can take more specific, even controversial positions than the OS Hub brand voice would typically use. An individual author can say “I think the industry is getting this wrong” in a way that the OS Hub company voice would not. This is a feature, not a risk — use it intentionally.

The OS Hub blog lives on WordPress and is our primary long-form content channel. It supports SEO, thought leadership, and stakeholder education.

### **Length & Structure**

* Target: 500–800 words per post.

* One topic per post. If you have two ideas, write two posts.

* Draft a title (or two options), outline sub-headers, then write.

* Use Classic editor in WordPress by default; switch to Block editor only for tables or embedded video.

* Run the Yoast SEO check before publishing.

### **SEO**

Each post should be optimized for one primary keyword. Place it in the title, opening paragraph, at least one sub-header, and naturally throughout. Core keyword pool:

* Supply chain transparency

* Supply chain data

* Supply chain map

* Supply chain due diligence

* Open supply chain

*Note:  Use UberSuggest (credentials in LastPass) to research keyword volume and find related terms. This list is currently five keywords; it is being expanded to ten as part of the 2026 SEO KPI targets. Check with the Communications Lead for the current full list before publishing.*

### **Writing Best Practices**

* Apply the OS Hub Piano structure: Inspire → Explore → Engage.

* Tell stories — ground data points in real-world examples or case studies.

* Include screenshots or images where they add value.

* Link every data point or external fact to its source.

### **Focus Areas**

Posts generally fall into: Data (platform data and findings), Impact (case studies and user stories), Money (funding, economic arguments), and General (thought leadership, industry commentary).

### **Workflow**

* Draft in Google Docs.

* Submit via the Monday.com form (linked in \#comms-digital-marketing).

## **LinkedIn**

LinkedIn is our only social channel. It reaches sector peers, partners, funders, and potential contributors.

### **Format & Length**

* Posts can be longer and more explanatory than newsletter snippets — context is welcome.

* Links should appear at the end of the post body, or in the first comment (labeled “Comment Link” in Monday.com). Do not bury links mid-post.

* Use paragraph breaks liberally — dense walls of text perform poorly.

* “Slider posts” (carousel-style): export from Canva as a PDF and upload to LinkedIn as a document post, not an image.

* Emojis: use sparingly and purposefully. One or two per post is the maximum. Emojis can add warmth and scannability, but overuse undercuts our credibility. They are better placed mid-post or at the end, not in the opening line. Do not use emojis in news announcements or formal communications. Avoid emojis with skin tone modifiers, even in the default yellow.

### **Visuals**

Use the Canva LinkedIn templates: 2025 LinkedIn Templates (vertical), Social Post Templates — Martina (horizontal).

### **Hashtags**

* Include 3–5 relevant hashtags per post.

* Core hashtags: \#SupplyChainTransparency, \#OpenData, \#SupplyChainDueDiligence

* Add event/campaign hashtags (e.g., \#WEF26, \#RanaPlaza) when timely and relevant.

* Do not exceed 7 hashtags.

### **Coordination**

* For launches, partnerships, or campaigns: share draft in \#comms-digital-marketing and get sign-off before publishing.

* Partner co-posts: align on copy, timing, and whether to post simultaneously or tag each other.

* Content ideas: drop them in \#comms-laurens-content-treasure-trove.

* Avoid editing published posts: once a post is live, the LinkedIn algorithm treats edits as a signal that the content is less trustworthy, which reduces reach. Proofread carefully before publishing. If a factual error requires correction, add a comment rather than editing the post body.

* Tagging: tag partner organizations when announcing a mutual milestone or collaboration, but confirm with the partner before doing so. Do not tag individuals without their permission. Photos or names of team members should only be shared if the team member has consented. For major announcements, align on tagging and timing with partner comms leads in advance.

* OS Hub page vs. CEO’s personal profile: for major announcements and milestone moments, consider whether to post first from the OS Hub company page, or to have Natalie Grillon post from her personal profile and then reshare on the company page. Natalie’s personal posts often generate higher reach given her personal network. Coordinate in advance so the timing and framing are aligned.

## **Press Releases & News Announcements**

OS Hub does not routinely distribute press releases. Reserve them for truly significant organizational moments — major partnerships, platform launches, or announcements with broad sector relevance — and only when there is capacity to do them well. Instead, we publish “news announcements” on the info site and use them as source language for LinkedIn posts, partner communications, and other channels.

### **Structure**

* **Headline:** Clear, active, specific. Title case (exception to the general sentence case rule — see Section 4). Lead with the most newsworthy element.

* **Date line:** XX Month YYYY.

* **Opening paragraph:** Answer who, what, where, when, and why in two to three sentences. Most important fact first.

* **Body:** Context, data, and supporting detail. Short paragraphs (three to four sentences).

* **Quote:** From OS Hub leadership (CEO: Natalie Grillon, unless otherwise specified). Adds perspective, not repeated facts.

* **CTA section:** Three to four clear calls to action with descriptive hyperlinks.

* **Boilerplate:** Close with the approved “For third parties to use” boilerplate from Section 3 (adapted to third person).

* End with “ENDS” in caps on its own line.

### **Partner Version**

For major announcements involving partners, create a second version they can adapt with their own quote, branding, and context. Provide as a Google Doc.

### **Tone & Approval**

* Formal, third person throughout.

* Specific and data-rich — numbers are more credible than adjectives.

* All news announcements require sign-off from the CEO or Communications lead before publication. Each announcement has a designated comms lead who owns the draft and coordinates approvals — this may be the Communications Lead, the CEO, or another relevant team member depending on the subject matter.

* For partnership announcements, the partner should also review and approve the shared language.
